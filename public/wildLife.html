About

The Sri Lankan leopard ( Panthera pardus kotiya ) is a remarkable subspecies native to Sri Lanka, an island country in South Asia. First described in 1956 by Sri Lankan zoologist Paules Edward Pieris Deraniyagala, this majestic big cat holds a significant place in Sri Lanka's biodiversity.

This leopard subspecies is distinguishable by its tawny or rusty yellow coat adorned with dark spots and close-set rosettes. Both males and females exhibit a range of sizes, with the largest males weighing up to 170 pounds and measuring around 4 feet in length, including their tail. Females are slightly smaller, with a weight of up to 124 pounds and a length of about 3 feet 5 inches.

As an apex predator, the Sri Lankan leopard plays a crucial role in maintaining the ecological balance in various habitats throughout the island. It is found in a diverse range of environments, including arid zones, dry zones, and wet zones, from protected reserves to unprotected areas. Leopards in Yala National Park, one of their prominent habitats, are known for their solitary hunting behavior, except for females with young cubs. They are agile and stealthy, silently stalking their prey before launching a swift attack to capture it. Their diet is versatile, including small mammals, birds, reptiles, and larger animals like axis deer, sambar, and wild boar.

However, the Sri Lankan leopard faces numerous threats to its survival. Habitat loss and fragmentation due to human encroachment pose significant challenges. Additionally, they often fall victim to snares set for other species and may be killed in retaliation for livestock depredation.

Efforts are being made to conserve this precious subspecies, with ongoing research and targeted conservation measures. Organizations like the Wilderness and Wildlife Conservation Trust (WWCT) and the Sri Lanka Wildlife Conservation Society are actively working with the government to safeguard the leopards' habitat and raise awareness about their importance in the ecosystem.

In captivity, there are 75 Sri Lankan leopards residing in zoos worldwide, with breeding programs and conservation efforts managed through the European Endangered Species Programme.

Overall, the Sri Lankan leopard represents a vital aspect of Sri Lanka's rich wildlife heritage, and concerted efforts are essential to ensure its survival and ecological contribution in the years to come.
