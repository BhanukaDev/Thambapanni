Sri Lankan Culture

Sri Lankan culture is known for its regional diversity and mixes modern elements with traditional aspects. Sri Lankan culture has long been influenced by the heritage of Theravada Buddhism passed on from India, and the religion's legacy is particularly strong in Sri Lanka's southern and central regions. South Indian cultural influences are especially pronounced in the northernmost reaches of the country. The history of colonial occupation has also left a mark on Sri Lanka's identity, with Portuguese, Dutch, and British elements having intermingled with various traditional facets of Sri Lankan culture. Additionally, Indonesian culture has also influenced certain aspects of Sri Lankan culture. Culturally, Sri Lanka possesses strong links to both India and Southeast Asia. Sri Lanka is a land of great cultural diversity. Religion pervades many aspects of life and constitutes a basic element of this diversity. Buddhist and Hindu temples, as well as mosques and churches, with their own colourful rituals, are the most readily visible features of the cultural landscape. Here are some things to know about Sri Lanka's local culture: - Sri Lanka is a multi-ethnic and multi-religious country. - The majority of people in Sri Lanka are Buddhists. - The Sinhalese people are the largest ethnic group in Sri Lanka. - The Tamil people are the second-largest ethnic group in Sri Lanka. - The official languages of Sri Lanka are Sinhala and Tamil. - Rice and curry is the staple food in Sri Lanka. - Tea is one of the most important exports from Sri Lanka. - Cricket is the most popular sport in Sri Lanka. - The traditional dress for women in Sri Lanka is the sari. - The traditional dress for men in Sri Lanka is the sarong.

Explore the breathtaking beaches, lush tea plantations, and ancient historical sites. Experience the vibrant traditions, delectable cuisine, and traditional dance forms that showcase the island's unique identity.

Whether you seek adventure, relaxation, or cultural immersion, Sri Lanka offers an unforgettable experience for every traveler.

Traditions and Customs

Sri Lanka's traditions and customs are deeply rooted in its religious and cultural diversity. The island is home to various religious festivals, each celebrated with fervor and enthusiasm. The Sinhala and Tamil New Year, Vesak, and Diwali are some of the major festivals that bring communities together in celebration.

The Sri Lankan hospitality, often referred to as "Atithi Devo Bhava," is a core value embedded in the culture. Visitors are warmly welcomed with open arms and treated as honored guests.

Traditional Dance

Sri Lankan traditional dance forms are a captivating expression of the island's cultural heritage. Each dance style narrates ancient stories and legends, and dancers don colorful costumes that add to the spectacle.

The Kandyan dance, featuring elaborate headdresses and rhythmic movements, is a must-see cultural performance. Other traditional dance forms include the devil dance and the low country dance, each with its own unique characteristics.

Also known as Poson Paya, this is the second most important one amongst festivals celebrated in Sri Lanka by the Buddhists. Since it commemorates the advent of Buddhism in the island in the 3rd century, Poson Festival holds great historical significance too. Just as in Vesak, the celebrations include lanterns, pandals, and alms-stalls across the island in the true spirit of the Buddhist traditions and teachings. The grandest festivities are in and around Mihintale rock outcrop where Buddha's disciple Mahinda first preached Buddhist doctrines to King Devanampiyatissa.

This Weeks Special

Esala Perahera is a grand festival celebrated in Kandy, Sri Lanka, with elegant costumes and is held in July and August every year to pay homage to the Sacred Tooth Relic of Lord Buddha, which is housed at the Sri Dalada Maligawa in Kandy. The festival is one of the oldest cultural and religious events of Sri Lanka and has been taking place every year for a millennia until the pandemic put a halt to it for two years.
